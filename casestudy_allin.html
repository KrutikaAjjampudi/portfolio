01 The Story

Before There Was
A Brief, There Was
a story worth telling

ALLIN Sports didn't come to me with a brief. There were no slides, no objectives document, no competitive analysis. What there was, was a group of friends who met on a tennis court and decided they wanted to build something together — a tennis academy with ambitions that stretched from hosting local tournaments all the way to competing as an academy and eventually moving into merchandise.

The brief was: make a logo. What I understood, from listening to their conversations and watching how they talked about what they were building, was that the brief wasn't really about a logo at all.

Context

A group of friends who met playing tennis. Now building a tennis academy together. The business goal is to create a space for tournaments — a stage for serious competition — while holding onto the warmth of the community that started it.

Their audience isn't defined by age. It's defined by love for the sport and the talent to pursue it. That distinction matters — it's what separates ALLIN from a club and makes it a brand.

There was no brief. Only a project. And sometimes that's more honest than a brief ever is.

The absence of a formal brief meant the design process had to start with listening. I gathered everything I needed from conversations — not between me and the client, but among the clients themselves. What they said to each other about their ambitions told me more than any brief would have.

02 The Name

When the Name
Does the
strategic work

Before any visual exploration, the name needed to be understood — not just read. ALLIN is not a descriptor. It's a declaration. And that distinction changed everything about how the identity was approached.

What ALLIN means in this context
ALL
IN.
No Holding Back
A statement of intent from founders who left nothing on the table when they decided to build this. The name is a commitment, not a concept.
Total Commitment
To the sport. To each other. To what they're building. ALLIN is how they play tennis and how they run a business — the same energy, no separation.
The Design Brief
Once I understood what ALLIN meant, the visual direction wasn't a choice to make — it was already decided. Bold. Graphic. No hedging. No safe middle ground.

This is the moment the instinct-driven process reveals its logic. I didn't explore multiple directions because the name had already chosen one. The only question was how to execute that direction with enough discipline to make it feel inevitable, not obvious.

03 The Process

Instinct Is Not
the absence of thinking

The process here was non-linear and instinct-driven — and I'd defend that choice. In a traditional agency context, you'd present three directions and workshop to a decision. That process exists to manage risk and build client confidence. For ALLIN, where there was no formal brief and the founders had trusted me completely, the right process was different.

01
Listen & Absorb
Before any design work, I spent time understanding the founders — not through a brief, but through observation. What they talked about, how they talked about tennis, what they wanted ALLIN to become.
02
Decode the Name
Spent time unpacking what ALLIN meant as a statement of intent before touching any tools. The name was the brief. Everything else followed from understanding it fully.
03
Build the System
Moved directly to execution with conviction. Combination mark — wordmark and symbol together. Bold, graphic, editorial. Then extended it into a full visual deck without being asked.

The visual deck was self-initiated. It wasn't in the brief — there wasn't a brief. But the logo alone felt like leaving the story half told. The deck exists to show ALLIN what they could become — the aspirational version of the brand they're still growing into.

04 The Decisions

Every Decision
Had One
test to pass

Every visual decision was run through a single test: does this feel like a brand that means ALLIN, or does it hedge? The combination mark, the colour choices, the typographic weight — all of it passed or failed on that basis.

The most significant structural decision was the mark itself — how the wordmark and symbol would relate to each other, and what each would carry.

What was rejected
Standalone Symbol
A separate abstract symbol that could function independently of the wordmark. Rejected because ALLIN is a name with meaning — stripping that meaning from the identity at the mark level would undermine the whole strategic foundation. The name is the power. The mark needed to carry it.
What was chosen
Combination Mark
A combination mark where wordmark and symbol work together and separately — but where the name is always present, always readable, always doing its strategic job. The symbol amplifies the wordmark; it doesn't replace it. For a brand at this stage, legibility of the name is more valuable than abstract iconography.

The decision to go bold and graphic rather than clean and minimal was equally deliberate. Tennis has a visual culture that defaults to refinement — white, understated, country club. ALLIN is the opposite of that. Their audience is defined by talent and love for the sport, not by the aesthetic codes of the sport's establishment. The visual language had to reflect that gap.

The editorial direction — typographically driven, high contrast, magazine-like in composition — came from thinking about where the brand was going, not just where it was. A brand with aspirations toward merchandise and tournament competition needs a visual system that can scale into those contexts. Editorial works at scale. Minimal often disappears.

06 Reflection

What This Project
changed

The instinct-driven process
I'd defend the approach taken here. Not having a brief wasn't a gap — it was an invitation to do the strategic work that a brief would normally have done for me. Listening, synthesising, deciding — that's the work. The output being strong on the first pass wasn't luck; it was the result of understanding the problem before picking up a tool.
What this project changed about briefs
Every brief I've worked with since ALLIN I treat as a starting point, not a constraint. The real brief is always behind the written one — it's in how the client talks about their work, what they're proud of, what they're afraid of. Learning to find that on a project with no formal brief made me better at finding it when there is one.
Designing for where they're going
The visual deck was built for a version of ALLIN that doesn't fully exist yet. That was intentional. A brand identity should give a young brand something to grow into — not just something that fits right now. The deck shows tournament posters, merchandise, social presence. None of that exists yet. But it will.
What I'd do differently
I would present the visual deck more formally — as a proposition, with a verbal framing of the brand strategy it was built on. The work was strong but it was handed over informally, which meant its strategic value wasn't fully read. The presentation of work is part of the work.
